# Planning Framework

Use this reference when creating a structured nutrition, fitness, weight-change,
or habit plan.

## Intake checklist

Collect only what is needed for the plan:

- Goal: health, strength, endurance, fat loss, muscle gain, maintenance,
  performance, energy, digestion, sleep, consistency, or confidence.
- Current baseline: age range, sex if relevant, height, weight, training age,
  weekly activity, job activity, sleep, and current routine.
- Constraints: schedule, equipment, budget, cooking ability, food preferences,
  dietary restrictions, allergies, injuries, medical conditions, medications,
  and travel.
- Tracking preference: no tracking, photo/plate method, portions, calories,
  macros, wearable data, workout log, or habit checklist.
- Success criteria: what should improve in 2 weeks, 8 weeks, and 6 months.

## Nutrition plan structure

Prefer this order:

1. Establish meal rhythm and protein anchor.
2. Add fruit, vegetables, legumes, or whole grains for fiber and micronutrients.
3. Match carbohydrates to activity, preference, and energy.
4. Include fats for satiety and dietary quality.
5. Adjust portions based on progress, hunger, energy, training performance, and
   adherence.

Use calorie or macro targets only as estimates. If estimating:

- Make assumptions explicit.
- Use ranges rather than false precision.
- Prefer a modest deficit for fat loss and a modest surplus for muscle gain.
- Avoid targets that require the user to ignore hunger, fatigue, menstrual
  disruption, poor sleep, or declining training performance.

## Fitness plan structure

Build from the user's current baseline:

1. Choose the minimum effective weekly schedule.
2. Include resistance training for major movement patterns: squat or leg press,
   hinge, push, pull, carry or trunk work, and unilateral work when useful.
3. Include cardiovascular training appropriate to the goal and recovery
   capacity.
4. Add warm-up and mobility only where it improves readiness or addresses a
   limitation.
5. Progress gradually by reps, load, sets, density, range of motion, or
   difficulty.

Default progression:

- Beginner: learn movement quality, leave reps in reserve, add consistency
  before intensity.
- Intermediate: use planned progression, deload when performance and recovery
  decline.
- Advanced: be specific to sport or physique goal and manage fatigue actively.

## Habit design

Make habits small enough to repeat on bad days:

- Define the trigger: time, place, preceding action, or calendar event.
- Define the action: visible, measurable, and under 10 minutes when starting.
- Reduce friction: prep food, pack gym bag, block calendar, remove distracting
  defaults.
- Review weekly: keep, shrink, swap, or scale.

## Plan review

Use a weekly review loop:

- Adherence: what percentage of the plan happened?
- Recovery: sleep, soreness, energy, mood, resting heart rate if tracked.
- Performance: strength, endurance, steps, workout completion, or pace.
- Nutrition: hunger, cravings, digestion, food satisfaction, and social fit.
- Outcome trend: weight average, waist, photos, labs from clinician, symptoms,
  or goal-specific markers.

Adjust one or two variables at a time so the user can tell what helped.
